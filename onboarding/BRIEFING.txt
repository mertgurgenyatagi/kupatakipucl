CHAPTER ZERO: HOW THE USER INTERACTS WITH CLAUDE CODE


Mert runs about a dozen active repos that span two very different registers. On one side: serious engineering — a JAX-based GPU reinforcement-learning environment for a custom card game, a real-estate CRM (konutrota) with a live dev database, a statistical bracket-simulation tool, and EventPortal, a scraping/aggregation pipeline that fetches, dedupes, and prices hundreds of events across nine Turkish ticketing sites. On the other: personal, low-stakes projects for friends — a World Cup prediction-pool site (Kupatakip) and a trivia app — written with Turkish mixed into the English, and clearly built for fun rather than production rigor.

His communication style is terse and imperative once a task is underway: "Go ahead," "Commit and push," "Argentina won. Log it," "Probe B then report back." Frustration and enthusiasm both come through directly and colloquially — "Now. Go get Biletino, tiger. I don't care what it takes," "Holy shit. Paraguay won. Log it," "Look Claude. This is what I want. It's really not that difficult." He isn't precious about phrasing; he wants forward motion.

But that terseness is earned, not default. For ambiguous or high-stakes decisions he explicitly gates the assistant before any action: "DO NOT GET STARTED. simply inquire," "tell me, don't do anything yet," "don't code or plan, just suggest." For big or unfamiliar builds he sets up his own iterative process — e.g. a self-designed 10-question requirement questionnaire, repeated round after round, with an explicit ask to keep questions non-technical because he's not an ML expert — and he invokes plan mode deliberately for large scoped work, wanting proposals and rated tradeoffs before implementation begins.

Autonomy is calibrated per repo, not globally, and he expects those calibrations to persist as standing rules across sessions and compactions. In his personal site repo: "push after every change," no need to ask each time. In the CRM touching a live database: "never run git commands unless explicitly asked" — stated as a "golden rule" he repeats and expects remembered. He treats git/deploy risk as contextual to what's actually at stake in each repo, not a fixed policy.

He's also a hands-on verifier, not a rubber-stamper: on EventPortal he personally spot-checked roughly half of a 262-event price export against live pages, and when he hands back corrections he wants root-cause fixes to the methodology, not individual rows patched — a code-only "tested against live data" claim has burned him before. He's comfortable with long sessions and background/async work, monitoring scraper and batch-scoring jobs mid-conversation while other work continues.

Finally, he treats Claude as a reasoning partner, not just an executor — he'll push back on conclusions ("I respectfully disagree... am I going crazy? I mean, you're an AI") and expects a substantive response, not deference.



CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION


This is another installment of a previous project of mine called "#kupatakip". For the sake of a fresh start, the previous repo was not included. 

The main mission statement of this project is this:

The UEFA Champions League starts at September 8. The teams to participate in the league phase will be determined by August 26. The tool must be ready by that date.

Between August 26 and September 8, a number of participants (was 19 at the previous edition, expected to be around 30 this time) will submit their guesses for how the league phase rankings will turn out. After September 8, the submissions will be closed. Depending on how the league phase actually turned out at the end, the participants will be scored. The scoring logic is as follows:

We go through every team and if the absolute difference between the predicted position of that team and actual position of that team is less than 3, the participant gets 3 points.

The league phase will be concluded at January 27. We skip the RO16 playoffs. The draw for the RO16 will take place on February 26. The RO16 will begin on March 9. Between February 26 and March 9, the second round of submissions will be open. These predictions will be completely seperate from the first round, meaning participants will use the confirmed bracket for the RO16 and onwards. At March 9, the submissions will be closed. Depending on how the knockout system progresses, the participants will be scored. The scoring logic is as follows:

For every team a participant has correctly predicted to be in the quarter finals, they earn 3 points. For every team a participant has correctly predicted to be in the semi finals, they earn 4 points. For every team a participant has correctly predicted to be in the final, they earn 5 points. If a participant correctly predicted the champion, they earn 6 points.
